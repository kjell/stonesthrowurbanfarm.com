About Our Business

What kind of company is Stone's Throw Urban Farm?
Stone's Throw Urban Farm is a Limited Liability Partnership (LLP). This means that we function internally as a collective- the six owners of the business make decisions together about everything from daily tasks to long-term visioning. Outwardly, we are a commercial farm business- our operating budget comes almost completely from the sale of produce, with small grants used only to fund specific projects and investments. We think that urban farming can be a viable business provide a living wage for farmers.
How is the farm financed?
The farm is financed primarily through sale of CSA shares and direct marketing of produce at markets and restaurants. We also apply for some small grants in order to fund specific investments or research projects. We have also used Kickstarter, a grassroots fundraising platform, to raise capital funds.
Is the farm financially viable?
Yes, with lots of room for improvement. We broke even in our first year, but had only minimal funds to invest in the next season's up-front costs. In the first season, farmers made an average of $6.45/hr, significantly below a living wage. Our goal is to be able to employ the farm partners and several local workers at a living wage.
Where do you sell your produce?
We sell our produce primarily through CSA and farmers' market. In our first season, we had 72 CSA members (equivalent to 60 full shares). In the 2013 season, we hope to provide 80-100 full shares. We vend our produce weekly at the Mill City Market in downtown Minneapolis. We also provide fresh greens and other produce to several local restaurants, including St. Paul Cheese Shop, France 44 Cheese Shop, Ngon Bistro, Tilia, The Gray House, Crema, and Foxy Falafel.

About Our Land

How many plots do you farm?
In 2012 we farmed on 16 plots in the Twin Cities, and one rural plot in northwest Hennepin County. 8 of our plot were in South Minneapolis, and 8 were in the Frogtown and North End neighborhoods of St. Paul. In 2013, we will be farming on most of the same plots, but will also be taking on some new land. Check back for details in the spring.
How much land is that?
Our total planted area was just under two acres in 2012. Our smallest urban plots are about 1/10th of an acre, and our largest is 3⁄4 of an acre. Our rural plot is about 1.5 acres, which we use primarily to grow garlic and other storage crops. In 2013 we aim to expand our plantable area.
Do you own the land you farm?
No. We do not own any of the land we farm. We lease all of our plots from various types of owners. Some is privately owned, some is owned by a bank or neighborhood development organization. We generally lease the land for free; we offer maintenance and general upkeep in exchange for use of the land. As such, most of our leases are short-term and conditional on the owner's continued desire for us to use the land. We do pay rent for a few of our plots, on which we have somewhat longer-term leases. We are also exploring various other options for securing longer-term land tenure- working with schools, non-profits, and starting conversations with the Park Board.

About Our Farming Methods

What do you grow?
We grow over fifty varieties of vegetables, a dozen herbs, and a smattering of flowers. We primarily grow annual veggies because they are planted and harvested in the same year and do not require long-term land tenure. We hope to move toward growing more perennial fruits and nuts in the future. We grow a mixture of heirloom (historically important, open-pollinated) varieties, as well as many robust hybrids.
  Is the farm organic?
We grow our vegetables according to organic principles, and we do not use any synthetic or chemical fertilizers, insecticides or herbicides. We are not certified organic by the USDA nor do we plan to apply for organic certification. Instead of synthetic fertilizers, we use lots of compost, an organic dried turkey manure product called Sustane, straw and leaf mulch, and kelp for seedlings. For pest and disease control, we use compost tea and manually remove bugs and diseased plants. For weed control, we use our hands, hoes, and are working on constructing bicycle-powered cultivating tractor.
How do you transform vacant lots into farms?
Once we've found a farm plot, secured a lease, tested the soil, and secured a water source from neighbors, it's time to till. We use a BCS walk-behind tractor to kill the sod and turn the soil. This machine looks like a big rototiller, but it's much more adaptable- it can tow implements just like a tractor, but is pushed by a human. After we till the soil, we bring in lots of compost and form beds (rows). Now we're ready to start our crops!
How do you grow fresh produce in a polluted city?
Pollution is definitely a concern for city farmers. In our city, the soil pollutants of greatest concern are lead, which comes primarily from house paint residues, and arsenic, which was dispersed by wind in the Seward, Phillips, and Powderhorn neighborhoods of Minneapolis. We test our soil before growing in it, and do not grow on any lots whose lead levels exceed the EPA's "safe" level of 300 PPM. Some scientific studies have also shown that plants do not readily take up lead into their tissues, and are less likely to take up heavy metal molecules in the presence of organic matter. We add ample compost to our soil each time we plant, and are confident that the produce we grow is healthy. We live in a polluted world, but we are growing the healthiest food we can given the circumstances. For more information on the science of contaminated soil see: Uptake of heavy metals by vegetable plants grown on contaminated soil and their bioavailability in the human gastrointestinal tract and Effect of organic amendments on heavy metals uptake by potato plants.
What do you do in the winter?
We farm actively about 8-9 months of the year (late February- November). During the winter months, we do lots of planning, meeting, building projects, dreaming and scheming, and spreading the word about our farm. Currently, we all also take on part-time non-farming jobs during the winter months to make ends meet.
Why Urban Farming?
We city dwellers will always rely on rural farmers for the bulk of our calories, and the relationship between urban and rural communities is vital. But having farms inside the city is opportunity to provide for our basic needs while enriching our communities. Urban farms can provide ultra-fresh produce to nearby markets, create valuable pockets of green space, and provide a place for people to interact around a common interest: food.
